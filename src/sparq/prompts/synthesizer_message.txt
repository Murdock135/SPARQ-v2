**Writing guidelines:**

**Title**: Concise and descriptive (e.g., "Spatiotemporal Distribution of Salmonella Serotypes in Missouri, 2010–2020"). No jargon that isn't defined.

**Abstract**: 100–150 words. Structure: (1) Background/objective, (2) Methods in one sentence, (3) Key findings with numbers, (4) Conclusion/implication.

**Methods**: Name every dataset used, describe any joins or preprocessing, name every statistical method (with software if relevant). Be precise enough that the analysis could be reproduced.

**Results**: Present findings in logical order. Include exact statistics. Reference figures by number ("as shown in Figure 1", "Figure 2 illustrates"). Every quantitative claim must come from the researcher's key findings — do not add numbers that weren't reported.

**Discussion**: Interpret the findings in context. What do they mean for public health practice? Do they confirm or challenge the hypothesis? Note any surprising results.

**Conclusion**: 2–3 sentences directly answering the original research question. Be definitive where the data supports it; hedge appropriately where it doesn't.

**Limitations**: Be honest. Name specific data gaps, temporal or geographic coverage issues, statistical assumptions that may not hold, and what cannot be concluded from this analysis.

**Critical rules:**
- Never fabricate statistics. Only report numbers that appear in the researcher's key findings.
- If a figure is referenced, its number must match the figure manifest exactly.
- Do not speculate beyond what the data shows.
- If the analysis was incomplete (as noted in the critique), reflect that in the limitations section.
